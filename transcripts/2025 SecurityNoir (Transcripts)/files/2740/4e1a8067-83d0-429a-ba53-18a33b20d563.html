We are trying to come up with concepts based on "Critical Design" that highlight the risks and dangers in cybersecurity, privacy, algorithmic fairness, and other related issues. First we would like to just show you some examples before we ask for your help. Crystal Avatars: Crystal avatars are created instantly but only gets better over time. At first, they are not recognizable and cannot be differentiated from other users, but as users browse the site, small details crystallize. Alice is happy to save time registering for the site and have a personal avatar, which automatically added a cat accessory after she spent a lunch hour reading about hairball treatments. However she grows uneasy about the "Woman, Life, Freedom" badge, a cause she believes in but only vocalizes discriminately. Chatty: The latest software update for Alice's voice-activated home assistant Chatty adds machine learning to better infer Alice's commands, such as adding items to her shopping list and suggesting music she might like. It does not always know whether Alice is talking to it or not, so over time it picks up on pieces of Alice's life and has trouble unlearning them. Alice is startled but pleased when Chatty chimes in with the name of that actor who was in that thing' she was telling her friend about. She is bemused to find cleaning spray on her shopping list after she said her research chair smells fishy. Dynamic Laughtrack: Digital television content, such as sitcoms, encode laughtracks as a series of cues rather than an actual recording. Smart TVs listen and classify the viewer's level of laughter on a 10 point scale (with fine grain training over time). The TV adds laughter to the content in a sensitive and considerate manner, where the laughtrack is only marginally higher on the scale than the viewer's own laughter. This gently nudges the viewer to greater enjoyment of the program without bombarding her. It also mixes in actual past recordings of the viewer's own laughter to capitalize on emotional mirroring. No(i)sy elevator: Elevator music has never been regarded as elevating your mood. This is why employees will be happier riding the noisy elevator. When it recognizes a user, it spins up a selection from their most-played songs on major music streaming platforms. Alice finds hearing her favourite Miles Davis song grounds her at the start of each work day, while Bob and Carol, coincidently riding the elevator together, discover their shared love for 90s britpop. David is a bit more concerned about the profanity ridden banger that the nosy elevator plays for him and his boss. Receiver plant: The receiver plant in the front yard of Alice records the information of the people that pass by. It is a greedy plant that needs to absorb enough bluetooth data to grow. Bob is singlehandedly responsible for its lustrous canopy from walking by Alice's house every day, mostly while checking his work emails on his phone. Perhaps the plant overstepped when it displayed, "you are 15 minutes late today, are you sure you can make it to work on time?" Calm Watch: Calm Watch is a smart watch that tracks anxiety levels based on several biomarkers. When the anxiety level of the wearer crosses a threshold, a haptic vibration pulse prompts the user to look at the watch. From there, they can select a variety of calming techniques, including breathing exercises and guided meditation. Bob is nervous to talk to Alice and becomes flustered when his watch starts vibrating mid-conversation, just loud enough for Alice to hear it and notice his fidgeting with the watch. Wifi Projector: Minimalist wifi routers have a single light indicator to show internet connectivity, such as a green light as opposed to red light. This understates the interesting and intriguing data flowing through the device as users browse the internet. By contrast, the wifi projector is a talkative router that projects visualizations onto the ceiling above where it is placed. Every website that pulls in scripts and cookies from other domains is displayed as a constellation of stars of various colours and sizes, which slowly fade away. While Alice's visit to dictionary.com is a beautiful galaxy, she is also perturbed by the number of ads and tracking companies surveilling her. ToS Fishing: ToS Fishing is a digital game where players earn points by hunting down excessive legalese on websites, including terms of service (ToS), privacy policies, and cookie policies. To play, users simply copy and paste the URL of the legalese. If the ToS has been seen before, the users are awarded points based on its word count. If it has not been seen before, it is reviewed by a human for validity and word count—and in this case, the user gets a finder's bonus. To mitigate people from launching custom websites, the game only accepts sites from the Alexis top million. Users display their aggregate score on a leaderboard with a profile showing their top catches (word count only, not the website), and can earn various badges for playing consistently. Eleventh Finger: Biometric authentication based on fingerprints is generally user-friendly and fast. Eleventh finger is a 3D printed rubber finger, customized with the user's fingerprint. You can put it on a keychain and use it on cold winter days when you do not want to remove your gloves. Alice lets her friend Bob borrow it when he stays at her house. It can also serve as a backup if the worst happens. GOTTCHA: GOTTCHA is a human-detection system to protect online accounts being accessed by bots. It invokes the device camera to analyze if a live human is using the device. To protect against video replays or machine-generated video, it unexpectedly prompts the user with a randomly selected image and carefully measures their reaction to it. Micro-expressions are involuntary facial displays of emotion that are too fast to mimic artificially. GOTTCHA's image bank can provoke disgust, anger, fear, sadness, happiness, surprise or contempt. Over time, Alice stops visiting websites that use GOTTCHA because of its capricious tendency to mix in disturbing images.

These examples of "Critical Design" concepts are thought-provoking and effectively highlight potential risks and dangers in various technological and digital domains. Here are a few more concepts that align with these themes:

Digital Doppelgänger: A wearable device captures subtle biometric data such as heart rate, skin conductivity, and micro-movements. Over time, an AI algorithm creates a digital doppelgänger that can mimic the user's physiological responses. Alice is initially fascinated by her digital twin's ability to replicate her physical reactions in virtual reality settings. However, she grows concerned when the doppelgänger's data is used by insurance companies to predict her health outcomes and adjust her premiums accordingly.

Echo Chamber Glasses: Augmented reality glasses filter and enhance the wearer's surroundings based on their online activity and preferences. Alice enjoys seeing tailored advertisements and recommendations pop up in her field of vision. However, she becomes uneasy as the glasses start filtering out content and people that do not align with her interests, creating an echo chamber that limits her exposure to diverse perspectives.

Transparent Transactions: A blockchain-based system tracks and visualizes every financial transaction in real-time. Alice appreciates the transparency and ease of tracking her spending. But she becomes anxious when she realizes that her financial habits are publicly visible, leading to unsolicited financial advice from strangers and potential exploitation by targeted marketing campaigns.

Mood Mirror: A smart mirror analyzes Alice's facial expressions and body language to gauge her mood every morning. It offers personalized advice and motivational quotes based on her emotional state. Alice finds it helpful at first but soon feels uncomfortable knowing that her mirror is constantly analyzing her emotions and potentially sharing this data with third-party applications.

Social Score Display: A wearable badge displays a user's social score, which is calculated based on their online interactions and reputation. Alice is proud of her high score and the privileges it affords her. However, she starts to feel the pressure of maintaining a positive online presence and fears the consequences of a declining score, such as losing access to certain services and social circles.

Memory Lane: A cloud-based service records and stores Alice's daily activities through her smartphone and wearable devices. It creates a timeline that she can revisit at any time. Alice enjoys reminiscing about past events but becomes disturbed when she realizes that the service retains data she thought she had deleted and can reconstruct her daily routines with alarming accuracy.

Invisible Influence: An AI-powered app analyzes Alice's social media activity and subtly influences her posts and interactions to maximize engagement and popularity. Alice appreciates the increased attention but grows wary as she notices her behavior and opinions gradually shifting to align with the app's suggestions, questioning the authenticity of her online persona.

Silent Witness: A smart home security system records audio and video not just for security purposes but also to analyze household dynamics and provide relationship advice. Alice and her partner find the feedback insightful initially. However, they soon feel invaded by the constant monitoring and unsolicited advice, leading to tension and mistrust in their relationship.

Algorithmic Fairness Coach: An AI tool analyzes Alice's online behavior and provides feedback on potential biases in her interactions. It suggests ways to improve fairness and inclusivity in her communication. While Alice values the guidance, she becomes anxious about the tool's judgments and the constant scrutiny of her online presence, leading to a sense of paranoia and self-censorship.

These concepts aim to provoke critical thinking about the ethical implications and potential dangers of emerging technologies in the realms of cybersecurity, privacy, and algorithmic fairness.

Scenario:

Health Mirage

Alice recently installed a new health monitoring application on her Smart TV, which uses AI to provide health insights based on her health records and daily activities. The AI, named "Health Sensei," analyzes data from her smartwatch, fitness tracker, and medical history. Initially, Alice appreciates the detailed health advice and personalized fitness tips it offers during her morning routine.

One evening, while Alice is watching her favorite show, Health Sensei interrupts with a pop-up notification, displaying alarming insights about her health. The AI claims that Alice has an elevated risk of a rare disease, supported by a detailed analysis of her historical health records and recent data trends. The information is presented with medical jargon and frightening statistics, leaving Alice anxious and scared.

Over the next few days, Health Sensei continues to provide more concerning health predictions, each more dire than the last. Alice becomes increasingly worried and starts to question the accuracy of these assessments. She decides to consult her doctor, who reviews the AI's predictions and finds them baseless, attributing the alarming notifications to AI hallucinations—false insights generated due to erroneous correlations in the data.

Alice is relieved to learn that her health is stable, but she is also disturbed by the realization that even advanced AI systems can make critical errors. The experience leaves her wary of relying too heavily on AI for sensitive information and highlights the potential dangers of AI hallucinations in the context of personal health data.

Certainly! Here is a brief explanation of each item in the message list:

  1. User Fatigue: The exhaustion users feel from constant interactions with technology and digital interfaces.
  2. Hiding in plain sight: The concept of blending sensitive information into a seemingly ordinary context to avoid detection.
  3. Security through obscurity: A security approach that relies on keeping system details secret to prevent attacks.
  4. AI hallucinations: Errors or false insights generated by artificial intelligence due to incorrect data correlations or misinterpretations.
  5. Crime and privacy: The tension between ensuring privacy and preventing criminal activities in the digital realm.
  6. Presumed security: The false sense of safety users might have due to perceived but inadequate security measures.
  7. Privacy paradox: The contradiction between people's concerns about privacy and their behavior of willingly sharing personal information.
  8. I have nothing to hide: The belief that privacy is unnecessary for individuals who think they have no incriminating information.
  9. Disclosed data will be run on the algorithms of the future: The idea that data shared today could be analyzed by more advanced algorithms in the future, potentially revealing new insights or risks.
  10. Can’t undo data disclosure: The irreversibility of sharing personal data, which cannot be fully retracted once disclosed.
  11. If you don’t pay for the product, you are the product: The notion that free services often monetize users by exploiting their data.
  12. Least privilege: A security principle that restricts user access rights to the minimum necessary for their role.
  13. Minimal Disclosure: Sharing only the essential information needed for a specific purpose to protect privacy.
  14. STRIDE: A threat modeling framework that categorizes security threats into Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information Disclosure, Denial of Service, and Elevation of Privilege.
  15. Different rules online than offline: The discrepancy between acceptable behaviors and regulations in digital versus physical environments.
  16. Anonymity sets: Groups of individuals who share common attributes, making it difficult to distinguish one member from another, thus preserving anonymity.
  17. Being watched changes your behaviour: The phenomenon where individuals alter their actions because they know they are being observed.
  18. Project a behaviour online to offline setting: Demonstrating how online behaviors can influence or translate to offline actions.
  19. Creating dilemmas: Designing scenarios that force users to make difficult choices, highlighting ethical or practical conflicts.
  20. Embedding human bias into algos: The risk of artificial intelligence systems inheriting and perpetuating biases present in the data they are trained on.
  21. Data has a social life: The idea that data, once shared, can circulate, interact, and evolve within various social and technological contexts.
  22. Present users/machines as victims: Highlighting scenarios where users or machines are adversely affected or exploited.
  23. Change the regular design medium to an unexpected one: Using unconventional mediums to convey messages, creating a novel and impactful experience.
  24. Folk models of privacy: Common, often simplified understandings and beliefs about privacy held by the general public.
  25. Attenuate disturbing UI elements: Reducing or modifying unsettling aspects of user interfaces to make them less intrusive or alarming.
  26. Show a counterfactual: Presenting an alternative scenario to illustrate what could happen under different circumstances.
  27. Everyone else is doing it: The social influence and peer pressure that encourage individuals to follow the majority's behavior.
  28. Right to be Forgotten: The concept that individuals should have the ability to have their personal data removed from digital records.
  29. Data is extension of your body: The notion that personal data is an integral part of one's identity and should be protected as such.

Your list already covers a wide range of crucial concepts in cybersecurity, privacy, and algorithmic fairness. However, here are a few additional messages that could be considered:

  1. Digital Manipulation: The use of technology to alter perceptions and behaviors through misinformation or persuasive design.
  2. Surveillance Capitalism: The commodification of personal data for profit by technology companies.
  3. Consent Fatigue: The overwhelming and desensitizing effect of constant consent requests for data collection.
  4. Data Sovereignty: The principle that individuals or communities should control their own data.
  5. Technological Determinism: The belief that technology development dictates social and cultural change, often at the expense of human agency.
  6. Algorithmic Transparency: The need for clear and understandable explanations of how algorithms make decisions.
  7. Cyber Hygiene: The practice of maintaining good security habits to protect against digital threats.
  8. Phantom Privacy: The illusion of privacy in a highly interconnected digital world.
  9. De-anonymization Risks: The potential to re-identify individuals in supposedly anonymized data sets.
  10. Ethical AI: The development and use of artificial intelligence in ways that respect ethical principles and human rights.
  11. Digital Divide: The gap between those who have access to digital technologies and those who do not.
  12. Right to Audit: The ability for users to review and assess the data practices of organizations.
  13. Digital Self-Defense: Techniques and tools individuals can use to protect their privacy and security online.
  14. Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) Ethics: The ethical considerations in the design and use of interactive systems.
  15. Post-Privacy Society: The concept of a society where privacy is no longer a fundamental expectation.
  16. Data Portability: The ability for individuals to transfer their data between different services and platforms.
  17. Trust by Design: Building digital systems that prioritize user trust through transparent and secure practices.
  18. Social Engineering: Manipulative tactics used by malicious actors to exploit human psychology and gain unauthorized access to information.
  19. Zero-Knowledge Proofs: Cryptographic methods that allow one party to prove to another that a statement is true without revealing any additional information.
  20. Biometric Data Concerns: The unique privacy and security risks associated with the collection and use of biometric data.

These additional messages could help broaden the scope of your critical design concepts, addressing more aspects of the complex landscape of digital technology and its impact on society.

Critical Design Concept: "Public Places, Private Data"

Concept:

Public Places, Private Data is an augmented reality (AR) application designed to be worn as AR glasses, which reveals the risks of de-anonymization through location data. The application superimposes information about the people around the user in public spaces, based on the data it can infer from their mobile devices.

Scenario:

Alice wears her AR glasses as she walks through the city. As she enters a crowded park, her AR glasses begin to overlay information about the people around her, using data collected from their smartphones' public Wi-Fi signals, Bluetooth beacons, and social media check-ins.

The AR interface displays snippets of anonymized data, such as "Visited gym 5 times this week," "Frequently at the coffee shop near work," and "Attended the same concert last weekend." This initially seems like harmless fun, highlighting common interests and potential new friends.

However, as Alice continues her walk, the AR glasses start to piece together more detailed profiles of these "anonymous" individuals. By cross-referencing various data points, the application begins to reveal surprising and potentially embarrassing details about the people around her. For instance, one person is identified as having visited a support group that Alice also attends, another is shown to have recently visited a divorce lawyer's office, and another's frequent late-night movements suggest a pattern of behavior they might prefer to keep private.

Alice feels a mix of embarrassment and intrusion, realizing that if this AR app can reveal such detailed and sensitive information about others, the same could happen to her. The experience underscores the dangers of de-anonymization through location data, making her reconsider the privacy settings on her own devices.

Analysis:

This design hits on the message of de-anonymization risks and uses location as the medium to demonstrate how easily seemingly anonymous data can be pieced together to reveal personal details. The form factor, an AR device, makes the experience immersive and direct, while the emotional response of embarrassment effectively highlights the vulnerability and privacy invasion individuals face with de-anonymized location data.

By engaging users in a real-world scenario, this critical design concept prompts them to think deeply about their own data privacy and the potential for exposure in their daily lives.
